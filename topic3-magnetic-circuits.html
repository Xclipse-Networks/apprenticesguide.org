Section 3.0

Magnetic Circuits.

Just as electrons flow through a conductor, magnetic flux flows through a medium. We use circuit theory to calculate exactly how much flux is produced in a core.

3.1

The Magnetic Equation

Ohm's Law Analogy

$$\Phi = \frac{F_m}{R_m}$$
$$\Phi$$ Flux (Webers)
$$F_m$$ MMF (At)
$$R_m$$ Reluctance

3.2.2 Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux is the invisible lines of magnetic force that pass through all materials. It can be calculated if the magnetomotive force ($F_m$) and the reluctance ($R_m$) of the magnetic circuit are known.

The unit of magnetic flux is the weber (Wb). The symbol is Φ (phi).

3.3

Reluctance ($R_m$)

Reluctance is the opposition a material offers to the setup of magnetic flux. It is directly proportional to the length of the path and inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area and permeability.

$$R_m = \frac{l}{\mu_0 \mu_r A}$$
  • l = Length of magnetic path (m)
  • A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
  • $$\mu$$ = Permeability of the material

Core Efficiency

Selecting core materials with high relative permeability ($\mu_r$) minimizes $R_m$, allowing for maximum flux with minimal energy input.

3.4

B-H Curve & Hysteresis

Magnetic Saturation

As we increase MMF ($H$), the Flux Density ($B$) increases—but only to a point. Once all magnetic domains are aligned, the material is saturated. Further increases in current will not result in more flux.

Hysteresis Loss

"Energy lost as heat due to the friction of molecular magnets realigning."
